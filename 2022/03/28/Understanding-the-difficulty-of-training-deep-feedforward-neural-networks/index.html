Understanding the difficulty of training deep feedforward neural networks

Abstract

Our objective here is to understand better why standard gradient descent from random initalizaiton is doing so poorly with deep neural networks, to better understand these recent relative successes and help design better algorithms in the future.

We first observe the influence of the non-linear activations functions. We find that the logistic sigmoid activation is unsuitd for deep networks with random initalization because of its mean value, which can drive especially the top hidden layer into saturation.
Surprisingly, we find that saturated units can move out of saturation by themselves, albeit slowly, and explaning the plateaus sometimes seen when training neural network.
We find that a new non-linearity that saturates less can often be benefitical.
Finally, we study how activations and gradients vary across layers and during traing, with the idea that training may be more difficult when the singular values of the the Jacobian associated with each layer are far from 1.Based on these considerations, we propose a new initalization scheme that brings substantially faster convergence.

5 Error Curves and Conclusions

Serveral conclusions can be drawn from these error curves:

  • The more classical neural networks with sigmoid or hyperbolic tangent units and standard initalization fare rather poorly, converging more slowly and apparently towards ultimately poorer local minima.

  • The softsign networks seem to be more rebust to the initalization procedure than tanh networks, presumably because of their gentler non-linearity

  • For tanh networks, the proporsd normalized inintalization can be quit helpful, presumbly because the layer-to-layer transformations maintain magnitudes activations (flowing upward) and gradients(flowing backward).

The other conclusions from this study are the following:

  • Monitoring activations and gradients across layers and training iterations is power investigative tool for understanding training diffculites in deep nets.

  • Sigmoid activations (not symmetric around 0) should be avoid when initalizing from small random weights, because they yield poor learning dynamics, with inital saturation of the top hidden layer.

  • Keep the layer-to-layer transformatons such that both activations and gradients flow well appears helpful, and allows to eliminate a good part of the discrepancy between purely supervised deep networks and ones pre-trained with unsupervised learning.

  • Many of our observations remain unexplained, suggesting further investgations to better understand gradients and training dynamics in deep architectures.

The saturation is related with activation function and initalization. A good initalization can help network convergence fast.
